## 1. The brand at a glance

**Name (full):** AutoBody San Diego
**Name (short):** AutoBody SD
**URL:** autobodysandiego.com
**Lockup in the logo:** AUTOBODY (chrome metallic) / SAN DIEGO (with SD shield centered between)
**Tagline (primary, sits in the logo):** Collision · Paint · Bumper · Insurance Help
**Hero headline (homepage):** San Diego Auto Body Help, Matched to the Right Repair Solution

**One-sentence definition:**

> AutoBody San Diego is the regional repair-guidance portal that helps San Diego drivers identify the right kind of auto body repair for their vehicle, then routes them to the specialist who handles that exact kind of work.

It is **not** a body shop. It is the front door to a repair network.

## 2. Strategic position

AutoBody San Diego occupies one specific role in a four-brand ecosystem:

| Brand | Role in the ecosystem |
|---|---|
| **AutoBody San Diego** | Regional traffic-capture portal. Owns broad search terms. Routes traffic. |
| **BumperFix USA** | Bumper specialist. Receives the bumper repair pathway. |
| **PaintFix USA** | Paint specialist. Receives the paint repair pathway. |
| **CollisionFix USA** | Collision specialist. Receives the collision repair pathway. |
| **CrashFix USA** | Accident / insurance specialist. Receives the accident and claim pathway. |

**The portal exists to feed the specialists, not compete with them.** This is the single most important strategic rule.

What this means in practice:

- AutoBody SD never positions itself as a body shop, mechanic, or service provider.
- AutoBody SD never claims to "do" repairs. It **helps the customer figure out which repair they need.**
- AutoBody SD owns the broadest, most generic search terms ("auto body San Diego", "auto body repair San Diego", "auto body shop San Diego"). The specialist brands own narrower, intent-specific terms.
- The brand should feel slightly more official, regional, and authoritative than the specialist brands. The specialists are soldiers. AutoBody SD is the command center.

## 3. Brand purpose

**Why this brand exists:**

San Diego drivers waste time at the wrong kind of shop. Someone with a curb-scuffed bumper books an estimate at a collision shop. Someone with structural damage goes to a quick paint-and-detail place. The market is fragmented and confusing, and "auto body" is treated as one category when it is really four very different kinds of work.

AutoBody San Diego fixes that by being the first thing a confused customer finds — and the thing that asks the *right* question:

> "What actually happened to your vehicle?"

From that single question, every visitor gets routed into one of four repair pathways and matched with the specialist built for that work.

This is a **guidance brand**, not a service brand. The customer's first interaction is being helped to make the right decision — not pressured to buy.

## 4. Audience

### Who this brand serves

1. **The confused customer.** Just had something happen to their car. Doesn't know if it's a $300 fix or a $3,000 fix. Doesn't know who to call. Doesn't want to be sold to.
2. **The deal-hunter.** Suspects the collision shop is overcharging for what is really cosmetic work. Wants a second opinion or a faster, cheaper specialist path.
3. **The post-accident customer.** Just had a wreck. Insurance is involved. Feels pressured by the at-fault party's insurer to use "their" shop. Needs guidance, not another adjuster.
4. **The mobile-search local.** Typed "auto body repair near me" or "bumper repair Chula Vista" on their phone. Wants a clear next step in 30 seconds, not a directory listing.

### Who this brand does NOT serve

- Dealerships doing OEM warranty work.
- Fleet managers needing specialized commercial repair.
- High-end exotic / collector restoration customers.
- Anyone explicitly searching for a known specific body shop by name (they've already decided).

## 5. Customer promise

### The promise (one sentence)

> Send us photos. We will tell you what kind of repair you actually need, what a realistic range is, and who in San Diego specializes in that exact work — without pressure, upsell, or a phone tree.

### What the promise commits us to

- **Same-day response** on photo intake.
- **Honest direction** even when it means routing the customer *away* from a specialist (e.g., "this is small enough you can buff it out yourself").
- **Plain language.** No insurance jargon, no shop jargon, no upsell language.
- **One front door, four clear pathways, no dead ends.**

If a marketing decision violates one of those four lines, it is wrong — even if it would convert better in the short term.

## 6. Brand personality & tone

**Tone, in three words:** Clear. Local. Authoritative.

### How the brand talks

- **Plain, direct sentences.** Short over long.
- **Customer language, not shop language.** "Bumper is cracked" — not "thermoplastic fascia damage."
- **Helpful first, salesy never.** The brand sounds like a knowledgeable friend who happens to know the repair world.
- **San Diego–specific, not generic.** Mentions actual freeways (805, 5, 8, 15), actual neighborhoods (Chula Vista, El Cajon, Pacific Beach), actual local moments (Otay Ranch parking lots, Pacific Beach sun damage, base traffic in Oceanside).
- **Calm authority.** Never panicked, never urgent, never "ACT NOW" language.

### How the brand does NOT talk

- ❌ "Best auto body shop in San Diego!"
- ❌ "Call now for a FREE estimate!"
- ❌ "Don't wait — your car deserves the BEST!"
- ❌ "Premium repair experience for discerning drivers."
- ❌ Long paragraphs of feature-bragging.
- ❌ "We've been serving San Diego since…" (we're new — leaning on tenure we don't have is dishonest)

### Voice examples (use these as anchors)

> "Most San Diego drivers default to 'body shop' the second something happens. But if the only damage is on the bumper, that's a different kind of work — and a different price."

> "You send photos. We identify the repair type. You get routed to the specialist built for that work — with realistic timelines, clear pricing, and no pressure to over-repair."

> "Don't agree to anything until you read this."

> "A full body shop is overkill if the rest of the car is fine."

Notice the pattern: a clear assertion, then a reason, then a takeaway. No fluff.

## 7. Visual identity

### 7.1 Color system

These are the locked design tokens. They live in [`assets/styles.css`](../assets/styles.css):

| Token | Hex | Role |
|---|---|---|
| `--ink` | `#000000` | Pure black, used for footer and deep backgrounds |
| `--bg` | `#0a0a0a` | Default page background — slightly off-black, easier on the eyes |
| `--soft` | `#111111` | Section variant background |
| `--steel` | `#161616` | Card / panel surface |
| `--panel` | `#1a1a1a` | Elevated panel surface |
| `--line` | `#262626` | Default border / divider |
| `--concrete` | `#f5f6f7` | Primary text — metallic white |
| `--muted` | `#9aa0a6` | Secondary text |
| `--accent` | `#2d8fff` | Electric blue — primary accent |
| `--accent-bright` | `#4fa3ff` | Electric blue hover / highlight |
| `--accent-dark` | `#0a66e0` | Electric blue pressed / depth |
| `--danger` | `#ff2d2d` | Red, used only for the "repair slash" element |
| `--danger-bright` | `#ff5252` | Red hover state |

**Rules:**

- Black is the dominant surface. The brand lives on dark, never on white.
- Electric blue is the workhorse accent. Use freely for CTAs, accent words, key terms, links.
- Red is the **scarce** accent. One "repair slash" element per major section, maximum. Red is never used for body text, paragraph color, or buttons (the only exception is `.btn-danger` for genuine warning states, not marketing).
- White (metallic concrete) is reserved for text and the chrome reads in the logo.

### 7.2 Typography

| Use | Font | Weights |
|---|---|---|
| Headlines, brand marks, buttons | **Chakra Petch** | 500, 600, 700 |
| Body copy, paragraph text | **Inter** | 400, 500, 600, 700 |
| Eyebrows, tags, labels, monospace numbers | **JetBrains Mono** | 400, 500 |

**Rules:**

- Chakra Petch carries the brand DNA. **Never substitute** another display font.
- Inter handles all readable body copy. Never use Chakra Petch for paragraphs — it's a display face, not a reading face.
- Mono is the "utility voice" — used for eyebrow labels (`SAN DIEGO COVERAGE`), tags (`PATHWAY 01 — BUMPER`), and small metadata. It signals "this is a system, not a brochure."

### 7.3 Logo system

**Current state (v1):**

The master horizontal logo at [`assets/logo.png`](../assets/logo.png) is the chrome AUTOBODY / SAN DIEGO mark with:

- Chrome metallic lettering
- Electric-blue car silhouette
- "SD" shield badge centered between AUTOBODY and SAN DIEGO
- "Collision · Paint · Bumper · Insurance Help" tagline baked into the image

It works at banner sizes. At small sizes (mobile header, favicon, social avatar), the tagline becomes unreadable.

**Logo variants we still need (Session 2 deliverable):**

| Variant | Use case | Status |
|---|---|---|
| Master horizontal (chrome on black) | Hero, banner, social cover | ✅ Done |
| **Simple header lockup** (no tagline, larger AUTOBODY SAN DIEGO) | Site header on mobile, business cards | ⏳ Session 2 |
| **Icon mark** (ABSD shield or SD badge alone) | Favicon, social avatar, app icon | ⏳ Session 2 |
| **Favicon** (32×32, 16×16, ICO/PNG) | Browser tab | ⏳ Session 2 (currently using master logo, suboptimal) |
| **White-on-transparent** version | Light-background applications, print | ⏳ Future |
| **Single-color black** version | Invoices, plain documents | ⏳ Future |

**Until variants exist:**

- The master logo IS the favicon (acceptable but not ideal — tagline doesn't read at 32×32).
- The site header uses the master logo at 46px height. This works on desktop but the baked-in tagline is unreadable on mobile.

### 7.4 Logo usage rules

- **Always on dark backgrounds.** Never on white. If you must place it on light, switch to the white-version variant (when it exists).
- **Minimum height: 36px.** Below this, switch to the icon-only mark.
- **Clearspace:** at least the height of the "A" in AUTOBODY on all sides.
- **Never** stretch, recolor, add drop shadows, or rearrange the lockup.
- **Never** combine the logo with another brand mark in the same lockup (e.g., no "AutoBody SD × BumperFix" cross-branding).

### 7.5 Imagery direction

When real photos are used (future content):

- Vehicles in San Diego settings (coastal sun, freeway, neighborhood streets).
- Detail shots of the *damage type* (bumper scuff, peeling clear coat, crushed quarter panel) — not generic before/afters.

**Avoid:**

- Stock "happy customer with mechanic" photos.
- Generic shop-floor wide shots.
- AI-generated cars (the eye catches the wrong reflections, mangled badges).
- Stock photos of obviously not-San-Diego cars (snow, mountains, European plates).

## 9. The four repair pathways

Every page on the site reinforces the four pathways. These are the **canonical pillar names** — use these exact labels everywhere, including nav, footer, page H1s, and meta titles:

| Pathway | Specialist destination | Lead question the customer answers "yes" to |
|---|---|---|
| **Bumper Repair** | BumperFix USA | "My bumper is scratched, cracked, or dented." |
| **Paint Repair** | PaintFix USA | "My paint is peeling, faded, scratched, or damaged." |
| **Collision Repair** | CollisionFix USA | "My vehicle was hit or damaged in a collision." |
| **Accident / Insurance Help** | CrashFix USA | "I need help after an accident or insurance claim." |

There is also a **fifth implicit pathway** that always exists on intake forms:

> "Not sure — need guidance."

This routes to a human review and is the safety net for customers who can't self-diagnose. Never let a customer hit a dead end because they didn't fit one of the four buckets.

**Rule:** Always lead with the customer's situation. Never lead with the service name. People search problems, not solutions.

## 10. Brand relationship to the specialist network

### Current public position (v1, until specialist URLs are live)

The site does **not name** BumperFix / PaintFix / CollisionFix / CrashFix in customer-facing copy that an outside visitor would read on a service page. Internal routing language only: "routed to a bumper specialist," "routed to a collision shop," etc.

Exception: the homepage **does** name the four specialists in the "ecosystem" section. This is intentional — it positions AutoBody SD as the deliberate front of a real network, which builds authority.

### Once specialist sites are live

Two changes propagate across the site:

1. **Footer attribution line:**
   > "Repair pathways powered by BumperFix USA, PaintFix USA, CollisionFix USA, and CrashFix USA."

2. **Replace `#` placeholders with real URLs.** Every CTA that routes to a specialist currently uses `href="#" data-route="specialist"`. Search the codebase for `data-route="specialist"` to find them all — there are roughly 6 per page × 26 pages, so plan for a scripted swap.

### Never

- Position AutoBody SD as a sub-brand of any specialist. The portal is the parent.
- Use the specialist logos in the AutoBody SD header.
- Claim that AutoBody SD performs the repair work.
- Imply the specialists are competitors of each other — they are pillars of one network.

## 11. What this brand IS / IS NOT

### IS

- A regional repair-guidance portal.
- A SEO-first traffic-capture site.
- A routing layer that funnels broad search intent into specialist destinations.
- A platform that helps confused customers find clarity.
- A San Diego–specific brand (the geography is the differentiator).

### IS NOT

- A body shop.
- A mechanic.
- A lead-gen marketplace (we route to one specialist per pathway, we don't auction leads).
- A national brand (it's deliberately bounded to San Diego — that's the moat).
- A directory or listings site.

## 12. Conversion voice

### CTA rules

| Use | Avoid | Use |
|---|---|---|
| Primary CTA | "Submit" / "Get Free Quote" | "Send Photos for Repair Direction" / "Get a Quote" |
| Secondary CTA | "Learn More" | "Choose Your Repair Type" / "See How It Works" |
| Routing CTA | "Click Here" | "Continue to BumperFix →" |
| Footer CTA | "Contact Us" | "Get a Quote" |

### Form rules

- **Labels** are in mono, uppercase, spaced — they should feel like a tool / dashboard, not a customer-service form.
- **Helper text** is in muted color, small, optional.
- **Submit button** is always the BIG primary action. Never two equally weighted buttons in a form.
- **Photos are first-class.** The form should make uploading 2–4 photos feel like the natural primary action, not an afterthought checkbox.

### CTA hierarchy on every page

1. Primary CTA: `Get a Quote` (or page-specific variant — "Get a Bumper Quote", "Get Accident Help").
2. Secondary CTA: `Choose Your Repair Type` / `Choose a Repair Pathway` / scroll anchor to the page's selector.
3. Routing CTA: `Continue to [Specialist] →` (one per page max, in the route-block section).

Never let a page exist without at least the primary CTA.

## 13. SEO posture

The brand identity has direct SEO consequences. Documenting the rules here:

- **Title tag formula:** `[Page topic] [City qualifier] — [secondary descriptor] | AutoBody San Diego`
  - e.g., "Bumper Repair San Diego — Scratches, Cracks, Dents | AutoBody San Diego"
- **Meta description formula:** First sentence states the topic in the customer's voice; second sentence names the routing or specialist.
- **H1 formula:** Service or topic + `<span class="accent">San Diego</span>` (or city name on city pages). Geography is always in the H1.
- **Internal linking:** Every service page links to (a) the quote funnel, (b) at least one related service page, (c) the service-areas hub, (d) the top 8 city pages. Every city page links to (a) all four service pages, (b) the quote funnel, (c) at least 8 other city pages.

(Full SEO strategy is a Session 11 deliverable; this is the lockdown that future content must match.)

## 14. Out-of-bounds: things never to do

1. ❌ Never write "Best body shop in San Diego." **This brand is not a body shop.**
2. ❌ Never use stock urgency ("LIMITED TIME!", "BOOK NOW!"). The brand is calm authority.
3. ❌ Never list more than four pathways on the homepage. Four is the structural commitment.
4. ❌ Never use a phone number as the primary CTA. The funnel is photos-first.
5. ❌ Never recolor the logo. It is chrome-on-black, period.
6. ❌ Never claim a specific repair shop is "the best." We route by repair type, not by reviews.
7. ❌ Never invent customer testimonials. If we don't have real ones, we don't show fake ones.
8. ❌ Never use AI-generated photos of cars. Real photos or no photos.
9. ❌ Never let a page have no CTA. Every page must have at least one outbound action.
10. ❌ Never use the brand name without "San Diego" in the same sentence on cold-traffic pages — the geography is part of the brand promise.
11. ❌ Never write "We are a leading…" or "trusted by thousands…" until those statements are demonstrably true.
12. ❌ Never reference a competitor by name.
